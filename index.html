    How the city defines a cuisine

    Short version: it doesn't, really. The cuisine label is not assigned by a city analyst against a rulebook — it is chosen by the restaurant.

    What the city's own data dictionary says

    The Health Department defines the field in exactly one sentence:

    “This field describes the entity (restaurant) cuisine. Optional field provided by restaurant owner/manager.”

    So the value is self-reported and optional. When a restaurant is permitted, its owner or manager picks one label from a fixed list of roughly 90 choices the city maintains. There is no published definition of what separates one category from another, and no review of whether the pick is “right.”

    Why that matters here

    • The categories are uneven by design. Some name a country (Chinese, Mexican), some a region (Caribbean, Eastern European), some just a food (Pizza, Donuts, Sandwiches), some a style (New American, Fusion). They sit in one flat list with no hierarchy.
    • Because it is optional, about 3,500 establishments leave it blank — they are real restaurants with no cuisine label at all.
    • A few labels are easy to misread. “Chinese” covers every regional Chinese cuisine; “Spanish” in this dataset means Spanish-Caribbean/Latino food, not food from Spain.

    That is why this page treats the labels as the city's own coarse, self-reported tags — useful at scale, but not an official taxonomy. Source: NYC Open Data dataset 43nn-pn8j, column data dictionary.

    ⚠ A caution about this map

    This was built with the help of artificial intelligence (Claude). Here is exactly what was and was not checked, so nothing is a black box.

    What is solid

    • Counts come straight from the city's published restaurant inspection dataset and are de-duplicated to unique establishments (one count per restaurant, not per inspection).
    • Borough and neighborhood totals were computed by direct query, not estimated.

    What to treat with care

    • “Cuisine” is an optional label the restaurant owner picks from a fixed city list — the Health Department does not define or vet the categories (see “How does the city define a cuisine?”). It is coarse: “Chinese” flattens every regional Chinese cuisine; “Spanish” in this dataset means Spanish-Caribbean/Latino food, not food from Spain.
    • The two groupings are an editorial call. Categories are split into “place of origin” (Chinese, Italian, Mexican…) and “food types & styles” (pizza, coffee, “American,” “New American,” “Fusion”…). Where a category names a style rather than a country — “New American,” “Fusion,” “Continental” — the call is a judgment, not a fact. You can map any category either way.
    • Neighborhoods are the city's 2010 Neighborhood Tabulation Areas. Some are large and blur famous enclaves (Koreatown straddles two; Little Italy is folded into a big SoHo-TriBeCa zone).
    • The dataset keeps some recently-closed establishments, so counts slightly overstate “open right now.”

    No claim of completeness is made. Verify any specific number against the source before publishing it.

    Methodology & sources

    Data source

    New York City Department of Health and Mental Hygiene, DOHMH New York City Restaurant Inspection Results (NYC Open Data, dataset 43nn-pn8j).

    How the city defines “cuisine”

    Per the dataset's data dictionary, the cuisine field is “an optional field provided by the restaurant owner/manager” — self-reported, picked from a fixed list of about 90 values the city maintains. The Health Department publishes no written definition distinguishing one category from another, which is why the labels are uneven and roughly 3,500 establishments are left blank.

    Counting method

    • The dataset has one row per inspection violation. Each establishment carries a unique CAMIS id. We count distinct CAMIS, so each restaurant is counted once regardless of how many inspections or violations it has.
    • Each establishment is assigned to a borough and a 2010 Neighborhood Tabulation Area as recorded in the dataset.
    • “Share of neighborhood” = a cuisine's establishments divided by all establishments in that neighborhood (any cuisine, including blank/unlabeled).

    Geography

    2010 Neighborhood Tabulation Areas, New York City Department of City Planning. Boundaries simplified for display.

    Known limitations

    • Cuisine is a single coarse, self/inspector-assigned label.
    • About 3,500 establishments have a blank cuisine field; roughly 600 are filed as “Other.”
    • Recently-closed establishments may still appear.
    • Neighborhood boundaries do not always match colloquial enclaves.
